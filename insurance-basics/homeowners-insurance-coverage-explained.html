Homeowners Insurance Coverage Explained

A homeowners policy is built from several separate coverage buckets. Each protects a different part of your home or financial risk. If you know what each bucket does, you avoid surprises during a claim.

This guide pairs well with dwelling coverage basics, personal property coverage basics, and liability coverage basics if you want focused breakdowns of individual coverage parts.

1. Dwelling Coverage

This pays to repair or rebuild your home’s structure after a covered loss: fire, wind, certain types of water damage, vandalism, and similar named perils. It applies to the physical structure—roof, walls, floors, built-in fixtures, and attached structures.

  • Based on rebuild cost, not market price.
  • Subject to your deductible.
  • Coverage requires the damage be from a covered cause.

If you’re unsure how your deductible affects payout amounts, see insurance deductible basics.

2. Other Structures Coverage

Covers detached items: sheds, fences, detached garages, and certain permanent yard structures. Usually set at a percentage of the dwelling limit.

3. Personal Property Coverage

This replaces your belongings—furniture, electronics, clothing, tools—after a covered loss. Special limits apply to jewelry, firearms, collectibles, and cash.

For a full breakdown of limits and replacement cost upgrades, read personal property coverage basics.

4. Liability Coverage

Liability protects you financially if you are held responsible for someone else’s injury or property damage. It does not cover your own injuries or your own property.

  • Slip-and-fall injuries on your property
  • Dog bites
  • Damage you accidentally cause to someone else’s property

If you’re unsure how much liability coverage to carry, see liability coverage basics.

5. Loss of Use Coverage

Pays for temporary living costs (hotel, short-term rental, extra transport or food expenses) when a covered loss makes your home unlivable.

More details are in loss of use coverage basics.

6. What Homeowners Insurance Usually Does Not Cover

  • Floods — requires separate flood insurance.
  • Earthquakes — usually requires a separate policy or endorsement.
  • Wear and tear, rot, deterioration.
  • Intentional damage.

For high-risk regions, review flood insurance basics and earthquake-insurance-basics.html.

7. Quick Annual Checkup

  • Review dwelling limits if you remodeled or added square footage.
  • Check personal property limits if you bought high-value items.
  • Confirm liability limits are not set to the bare minimum.
